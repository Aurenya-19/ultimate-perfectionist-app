üìñ Introduction

What are Newton's Laws of Motion?

Newton's Laws of Motion are three fundamental principles that describe the relationship between the motion of an object and the forces acting upon it. These laws form the foundation of classical mechanics and are essential for understanding motion in our everyday world.

Historical Context: Sir Isaac Newton published these laws in his work "Philosophi√¶ Naturalis Principia Mathematica" (Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy) in 1687. These laws revolutionized our understanding of physics and remain valid for most everyday situations.

Key Concept: Newton's laws apply to inertial reference frames - frames that are either at rest or moving with constant velocity. They don't apply in accelerating reference frames without modifications.

‚öñÔ∏è Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

Statement

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force."

Understanding Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. It is directly proportional to the mass of the object.

  • Inertia of Rest: Tendency to remain at rest (e.g., a book on a table)
  • Inertia of Motion: Tendency to keep moving (e.g., a moving car continues forward when brakes are applied)
  • Inertia of Direction: Tendency to maintain direction (e.g., passengers lean outward when a car turns)
Important: Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia. Greater mass = Greater inertia = More difficult to change motion.

üîç Real-Life Examples

1. Seat Belts in Cars: When a car suddenly stops, passengers continue moving forward due to inertia. Seat belts provide the external force needed to stop them.

2. Shaking a Wet Umbrella: Water droplets fly off because they tend to continue in their state of motion when the umbrella is suddenly stopped.

3. Pulling a Tablecloth: If pulled quickly, objects on the table remain in place due to inertia.

üöÄ Newton's Second Law (F = ma)

Statement

"The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force."

$$\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$ $$\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$

Where:

  • \(\vec{F}\) = Net force (Newton, N)
  • \(m\) = Mass (kilogram, kg)
  • \(\vec{a}\) = Acceleration (m/s¬≤)
  • \(\vec{p}\) = Momentum = \(m\vec{v}\)

üìê Derivation

Step 1: Start with momentum $$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
Step 2: Rate of change of momentum $$\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \frac{d(m\vec{v})}{dt}$$
Step 3: For constant mass $$\vec{F} = m\frac{d\vec{v}}{dt} = m\vec{a}$$
Note: If mass is variable (like in rocket propulsion), we must use \(\vec{F} = \frac{d(m\vec{v})}{dt}\)

üìù Example 1: Force Calculation

Problem: A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates from rest to 20 m/s in 5 seconds. Calculate the force applied.

Solution:

Given: \(m = 1000\) kg, \(u = 0\), \(v = 20\) m/s, \(t = 5\) s

Acceleration: \(a = \frac{v-u}{t} = \frac{20-0}{5} = 4\) m/s¬≤

Force: \(F = ma = 1000 \times 4 = 4000\) N

Answer: The force applied is 4000 N or 4 kN.

üìù Example 2: Multiple Forces

Problem: Two forces 10 N and 15 N act on a 5 kg object at right angles. Find the acceleration.

Solution:

Net force: \(F_{net} = \sqrt{10^2 + 15^2} = \sqrt{100 + 225} = \sqrt{325} = 18.03\) N

Acceleration: \(a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{18.03}{5} = 3.61\) m/s¬≤

Direction: \(\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{15}{10}\right) = 56.3¬∞\) from the 10 N force

Answer: Acceleration is 3.61 m/s¬≤ at 56.3¬∞ from the 10 N force.

‚ö° Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)

Statement

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

$$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$

Key Points

  • Action and reaction forces are equal in magnitude
  • They are opposite in direction
  • They act on different objects (this is crucial!)
  • They act simultaneously
  • They are of the same type (both gravitational, both electromagnetic, etc.)
Common Misconception: Action and reaction forces do NOT cancel each other because they act on different objects. Forces cancel only when they act on the same object.

üîç Real-Life Examples

1. Walking: You push the ground backward (action), the ground pushes you forward (reaction).

2. Swimming: You push water backward (action), water pushes you forward (reaction).

3. Rocket Propulsion: Rocket expels gases downward (action), gases push rocket upward (reaction).

4. Recoil of Gun: Bullet moves forward (action), gun recoils backward (reaction).

üìù Example 3: Recoil Velocity

Problem: A 5 kg gun fires a 50 g bullet at 400 m/s. Find the recoil velocity of the gun.

Solution:

Using conservation of momentum (from Newton's third law):

Initial momentum = Final momentum

\(0 = m_b v_b + m_g v_g\)

\(0 = (0.05)(400) + (5)(v_g)\)

\(v_g = -\frac{0.05 \times 400}{5} = -4\) m/s

Answer: The gun recoils at 4 m/s in the opposite direction.

üí• Momentum & Impulse

Linear Momentum

Momentum is the quantity of motion possessed by a moving object.

$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
  • SI Unit: kg¬∑m/s or N¬∑s
  • It is a vector quantity
  • Direction: Same as velocity

Impulse

Impulse is the change in momentum or the product of force and time.

$$\vec{J} = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta t = \Delta \vec{p} = m(\vec{v} - \vec{u})$$
Impulse-Momentum Theorem: The impulse applied to an object equals the change in its momentum.

Conservation of Momentum

"In the absence of external forces, the total momentum of a system remains constant."

$$\vec{p}_{initial} = \vec{p}_{final}$$ $$m_1\vec{u}_1 + m_2\vec{u}_2 = m_1\vec{v}_1 + m_2\vec{v}_2$$

Applications:

  • Collisions (elastic and inelastic)
  • Explosions
  • Rocket propulsion
  • Recoil of guns

üìù Example 4: Collision Problem

Problem: A 2 kg ball moving at 5 m/s collides with a 3 kg ball at rest. After collision, they move together. Find their common velocity.

Solution:

This is a perfectly inelastic collision (objects stick together).

Using conservation of momentum:

\(m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v\)

\((2)(5) + (3)(0) = (2 + 3)v\)

\(10 = 5v\)

\(v = 2\) m/s

Answer: The combined mass moves at 2 m/s.

Problem 4 Medium
A cricket ball of mass 150 g moving at 25 m/s is hit by a bat and returns in the opposite direction at 30 m/s. If the contact time is 0.01 s, find the average force exerted by the bat.

C) 900 N
D) 1000 N

‚úÖ Solution

Given: m = 150 g = 0.15 kg, u = 25 m/s, v = -30 m/s (opposite direction), t = 0.01 s

Using Impulse-Momentum Theorem:

F √ó t = m(v - u)

F = m(v - u)/t

F = 0.15 √ó (-30 - 25)/0.01

F = 0.15 √ó (-55)/0.01

F = -825 N

Magnitude = 825 N (negative sign indicates direction)

Answer: B) 825 N

Problem 5 Hard
A rocket of mass 1000 kg ejects gases at a rate of 10 kg/s with a velocity of 2000 m/s relative to the rocket. Calculate the thrust on the rocket.
A) 15,000 N
B) 18,000 N
C) 20,000 N
D) 22,000 N

‚úÖ Solution

Given: dm/dt = 10 kg/s, v_rel = 2000 m/s

Thrust Formula:

F = v_rel √ó (dm/dt)

F = 2000 √ó 10

F = 20,000 N = 20 kN

Explanation: Thrust is the force produced by ejecting mass at high velocity (Newton's third law).

Answer: C) 20,000 N
